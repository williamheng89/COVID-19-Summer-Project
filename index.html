    There is this syndrome called MIS-C which stands for Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children. It is a serious condition in some parts of the body-such as the heart, blood vessels, kidneys, digestive system, brain, skin, or eyes-become inflamed. Inflammation includes swelling, often with redness and pain.

    Signs and Symptoms of MIS-C Includes:

      Long lasting fever
      Vomiting
      Dirrahea
      Pain in the Stomach
      Skin rash
      Red eyes
      Redness or swelling of the lips and tongue
      Feeling unusually tired
      Redness of swellling of hands or feet

    Emergency Signs of MIS-C:

      Inability to wake up or stay awake
      Difficulty breathing
      Chest pain or pressure that doesn't go away
      New confusion
      Bluish lips or face
      Severe stomach pain

    Immune Response

    Upon the contraction of COVID-19, our bodies, depending on our age and past medical history, react and combat the virus. According to Immunologists and professors at Tufts University School of Medicine, the first immune response is called innate immunity which is a very rapid response to the virus. The purpose of innate immunity is to prevent viruses such as COVID-19 from spreading and moving throughout the body by recognizing the foreign bodies and producing cytokines. IL-1beta, a cytokine produced in response to COVID-19, is responsible for raising your body temperature in an attempt to kill the virus (Julie Flaherty). If the coronavirus does pass innate immunity, the body’s second line of defense called adaptive immunity will kick in. According to researchers of the Keck School of Medicine at USC, “The adaptive immune response employs what it has learned about the virus to mobilize a variety of special forces such as T cells and B cells.” B cells work by producing antibodies which attach to the invading pathogen and mark it for immune cells to destroy it while T cells work to kill cells that have been infected by the virus and stimulate B cells to produce antibodies (Cancer Treatment Centers of America).

    So if our immune systems function similarly, why do children and young adults have a better response to COVID-19 in comparison to older individuals? According to National Geographic and NBC news, approximately 80 percent of individuals aged 20 and under may be asymptomatic while only 35.4% of people over the age of 80 show no symptoms of coronavirus. In addition, 90 percent of pediatric patients tend to be asymptomatic, yet children under the age of five are hit harder by the virus than older children. Since Covid has slow disease progression and attacks the surface cells existing in the respiratory system, the adaptive immune response may begin too early, interfering with the innate immune system's ability to kill the virus off quickly. This period of prolonged viral existence can cause the immune system to overreact or undergo a cytokine storm which can kill healthy cells and damage tissue. In young children with underdeveloped immune systems or older individuals with weaker immune systems, the proper pathogens needed to fight off the virus aren’t available and immune systems don’t have enough energy to fight off coronavirus. But, unlike those of young children and older patients, the immune systems of young adults and children who do contract coronavirus “may reach a sort of just-right status, growing strong enough to keep an infection in check without overreacting” (Katherine J. Wu). So, for teens and children, a diagnosis of coronavirus is not necessarily a death sentence so long as you do not have underlying health conditions.
